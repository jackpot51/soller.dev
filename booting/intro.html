Booting: Introduction

How modern computers get from the power button to your desktop.

By Jeremy Soller

Computers are complicated machines, that themselves consist of many smaller machines. It would take many years to understand the operations of just one such component. This blog will attempt to explain the boot process from the point of view of the firmware and software, and will abstract some parts of the boot process for the purpose of simplicity. Bear in mind, that this simplification will still not make things simple.

Also, there is considerable variability among computers, their firmware, and the software that they run. In the past that has meant that the boot process could vary wildly, though it would usually have some common concepts. For modern computers and operating systems, the boot process generally uses Unified Extensible Firmware Interface (UEFI) and Advanced Configuration and Power Interface (ACPI), so this is what we will discuss.

With those stipulations out of the way, I'd like to first introduce the concept of booting from a high level perspective. A user presses the power button on a computer, the computer does something, a login screen is shown, the user logs in, and they can now continue doing whatever they planned to do with the computer. To most users, the whole process of booting - whatever time the computer takes to prepare itself to be useful - seems as innocuous as it seems vestigial. On the one hand, there is little reason for most users to care about something that happens so rarely. On the other hand, it seems with such powerful systems that they really ought to just boot straight to the desktop. What is so important that the computer spends billions of cycles on it, while the user waits?

It would be careless to say those cycles are not wasted. For sure, many of them are. But the truth about the boot process, is that our computer hardware has become so wildly complicated, running at the very edge of known physics, while at the same time computer firmware (and software) has absorbed as much code as programmers could fit into whatever storage holds it. This has led to boot times increasing over time, rather than decreasing, even if it should be possible to do the opposite.

It would also not be nice to say firmware developers do not care about boot speed. Many of them do. But the environment that we operate in is one where the performance of components at runtime is vastly more important than their performance during boot. Additionally, there is a desire to push out hardware as soon as possible, bugs in firmware are often tolerated if they do not affect the runtime experience. For example, the firmware having to initialize something multiple times is a clear "bug", but in the firmware world it may be seen as a "feature" if it works around some issue and lets hardware ship sooner.

Back to the point, we can guess the computer is doing something in that time, though it could probably do it better. We can guess that after the power button is pressed, a piece of code called "firmware" initializes some things, reads "software" from a storage device, and then transfers control to it. That software, the operating system, then has support for all the hardware on the computer, and abstracts it for other software to utilize. It is often not realized that the firmware, in many ways, resembles an operating system. It is composed of software, and has drivers for a wide set of hardware. The only real difference, then, is where it is stored.
